What the data protection bill changes — and what it doesn't

Parliament passed the long-delayed data protection law after a marathon overnight sitting. Here is what it means for the people whose data it governs — and the questions it leaves open.

India · Policy Analysis New Delhi 25 June 2026 · 8 min read
A
Arya Mehta Senior Correspondent · New DelhiUpdated 11:48, 25 Jun 2026

After nearly four years of drafts, withdrawals and redrafts, India finally has a data protection law. The bill cleared the Lok Sabha at a quarter to midnight, 248 votes to 119, and is expected to pass the upper house within the week. For a country that adds more first-time internet users than any other, it is a foundational moment — and a deeply contested one.

By the numbers
248–119

The margin by which the Lok Sabha passed the bill, just before midnight.

At its centre is a simple promise: that the companies and government departments holding your personal information must tell you why they have it, use it only for that reason, and delete it when the reason ends. Around that promise the law builds a scaffold of consent notices, a new regulator, and penalties large enough, in theory, to make the largest platforms pay attention.

What it does not settle is who watches the watchers. The same state that enforces the law also exempts itself from large parts of it — and that tension runs through every clause.

§What actually changes

Note · Definition
The Eighth Schedule

The part of the Constitution that lists India's 22 officially recognised languages. Consent notices must now be offered in any of them.

For an ordinary reader, the most visible shift will be the consent notice: a plain-language explanation, available in every language in the Eighth Schedule, of what an app intends to do with your data before you agree. Buried checkboxes and pre-ticked permissions are, on paper, finished.

  • A right to correction and erasure. You can ask a company to fix wrong data, or to delete it once it is no longer needed.
  • Consent in your language. Notices must be offered in any of the twenty-two scheduled languages, not English alone.
  • A Data Protection Board. A new body to hear complaints and levy penalties of up to ₹250 crore for serious breaches.
  • Limits on children's data. Tracking and targeted advertising aimed at under-eighteens is restricted.
Penalty ceiling
₹250cr

The maximum fine the new board can levy for a serious or repeated breach.

Taken together, these are real protections that did not exist a day ago. Privacy advocates who have fought for them since the Supreme Court's 2017 judgment recognising privacy as a fundamental right call the framework, at last, a beginning.

Context · 2017
Puttaswamy v. Union of India

The Supreme Court ruling that held privacy to be a fundamental right — the legal foundation this law is built on.

“

A right is only as strong as the body that enforces it — and that body is still being built.

Ananya Iyer, technology lawyer

§What it leaves open

The law's critics point to a single recurring word: exemption. Government agencies can be released from its obligations in the interest of national security or public order — terms the bill does not define narrowly. The board that hears complaints is appointed by the government it may be asked to investigate. And cross-border data transfer rules were softened in the final draft, to the relief of large platforms.

Read next A law that protects you from companies, but not the state →

None of this makes the law toothless. But it does mean the protections an individual can claim against a private company are stronger than those they can claim against the state — an asymmetry that lawyers expect to be tested in court within months.

For now, the practical timeline matters most. The Data Protection Board must be constituted, rules must be notified, and companies will be given a window — likely a year — to comply. Until then, the consent notice you are promised remains, like much of the law, a thing that exists on paper and waits to be built.
